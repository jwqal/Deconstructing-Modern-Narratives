Manufacturing Consent on the Margins

A Propaganda Model Analysis of Media Coverage of Bihar's Electoral Roll Revision

Introduction: The Representative Text and the Analytical Framework

In the politically charged landscape of India, the actions of the Election Commission of India (ECI), a constitutionally mandated body, are subject to intense scrutiny. This report analyzes media coverage of Bihar's electoral roll revision through the lens of Edward S. Herman and Noam Chomsky's Propaganda Model. The model posits that news is shaped by five systemic filters: ownership, advertising, sourcing, flak, and dominant ideology. Which work to manufacture consent for the agendas of dominant state and corporate elites.

The ECI is an autonomous constitutional authority responsible for administering election processes in India. Its independence is considered crucial for free and fair elections.
A conceptual model in political economy that argues systemic biases in mass media, stemming from economic structures, shape news content to be favorable to established power.

The representative text is an article from The Hindu, a prominent national "newspaper of record." This seemingly neutral, data-driven report on the procedural aspects of the revision is an ideal subject for deconstruction, as it reveals how ideological constraints operate not through overt propaganda but through subtle choices of framing, emphasis, and omission.

A "newspaper of record" is a major newspaper that has a large circulation and whose editorial and news-gathering functions are considered authoritative and of high quality.

This report will reveal the "story not told"—the narrative of political conflict, potential mass disenfranchisement, and fear that is systematically marginalized in the mainstream coverage.

The Sanitized Narrative: Reporting Process, Obscuring Conflict

The SIR in Bihar was a deeply divisive political event. Opposition parties launched massive protests, framing the exercise as "institutionalised chori [theft] to deny the poor their right to vote". There were widespread fears that the SIR was a "backdoor" National Register of Citizens (NRC), an exercise that had created immense anxiety in the state of Assam.

Hindi for "theft." By using this term, the opposition framed the administrative process not as a bureaucratic error, but as a deliberate, criminal act against voters.
An official record of all legal citizens of India. A similar exercise in the state of Assam excluded nearly 2 million people, raising fears of statelessness for many, particularly minorities.

None of this profound conflict is reflected in the chosen article. The article's focus on the how (the process) completely obscures the why (the battle over who gets to be a voter). This elevation of process over conflict serves to normalize a highly controversial state action.

Deconstructing the Narrative Through the Five Filters

1. The Ownership Filter: The Liberal Establishment's Cautious Gaze

As a "newspaper of record," The Hindu is invested in maintaining its reputation for sobriety and institutional respect. This creates a tension: its liberal leanings might align with critics, but its institutional imperative is to treat a body like the ECI with deference. This results in cautious, procedural coverage that avoids inflammatory language, ultimately affirming the state's narrative.

2. The Advertising Filter: The Price of Dissent

The Indian government is a major advertiser and has a documented history of weaponizing its ad budget to punish critical media. A 2019 Reuters report noted that ad spending to The Hindu was frozen after critical coverage. This creates a powerful chilling effect. The path of least financial risk is to stick to the official script, a path the article follows meticulously.

3. The Sourcing Filter: The Unquestioned Authority of the State

The article is built almost exclusively on information from the ECI. Official sources are granted an automatic presumption of neutrality. In contrast, the opposition's claims are treated as biased "political noise," and the lived experiences of ordinary citizens are considered anecdotal. This structural preference ensures the state's framing dominates.

4. The Flak Filter: The Shadow of Reprisal

In a context of shrinking press freedom, with journalists facing legal harassment, raids, and arrests, the anticipation of a ferocious backlash creates a powerful incentive for self-censorship. An entirely "safe" report that makes no independent claims and offers no critical analysis insulates the newspaper from the most severe forms of flak. The story is covered, but the controversy is contained.

"Flak" refers to negative responses (lawsuits, social media campaigns, official sanctions) that media face for critical content. The anticipation of flak can lead to self-censorship.

5. The Dominant Ideology Filter: The Sanctity of Bureaucratic Process

The story is framed by the ideology that "purifying" electoral rolls is a self-evidently good, apolitical task. Opposition is then delegitimized and framed as partisan actors defending "bogus voters" or "shielding infiltrators." The media's uncritical adoption of this frame manufactures consent for an exclusionary ideology by presenting the process as a value-neutral state function.

The Story Not Told: Narratives of Exclusion and Fear

The sanitized narrative omits the profound political context: the fear that the SIR was a trial run for a nationwide NRC. Also silenced is the human dimension—the experiences of groups most at risk, like migrant labourers, women (disproportionately deleted from rolls after marriage), and the poor, who often lack the specific documents required.

The narrative of "vote theft"—a direct and explosive challenge to the legitimacy of the entire exercise—is the core of the political story. Its complete absence is a testament to the power of the propaganda model's filters.

Conclusion: A Tripartite Summary

  1. The Dominant Narrative: The media presented a neutral, efficient administrative process, portraying the ECI as a competent authority ensuring electoral "purity."
  2. Crucial Omissions: The coverage systematically omitted the deep political controversy, fears of a "backdoor NRC," and the human cost of potential disenfranchisement.
  3. Centered vs. Marginalized Voices: The state's voice (ECI) was amplified as objective fact, while the political opposition and impacted citizens were marginalized or silenced entirely.
